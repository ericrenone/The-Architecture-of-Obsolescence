# The Architecture of Obsolescence
## Prestige Copying, Grade Inflation, Consumer Universities, and the Moral Reckoning Adults Owe Children in the Age of Machine Intelligence

> *"The planner over-invests in skills destined for obsolescence, a distortion that increases monotonically with AI prevalence."*
> — Peterson, 2025 (arXiv:2508.19625)

> *"The point of your college education was to serve you — your knowledge, your résumé, your personal growth. You had no obligations to the school. You were there to feed on the university."*
> — Michal Leibowitz, *The New York Times*, May 20, 2026

> *"A Harvard A grade will now tell them, as well as employers and graduate schools, something real about what a student has achieved. An A will once again be what Harvard's guidelines have long said it is: a mark of extraordinary distinction."*
> — Harvard FAS Grading Subcommittee, May 20, 2026

> *"Every child who wastes a year mastering what a machine already does perfectly is not failing. The adults who designed that child's incentive structure are."*

## The Central Claim

This is not a guide about artificial intelligence.

It is a moral document about adult responsibility grounded in empirical evidence.

The research is settled. The benchmark record is public. The labor market data is peer-reviewed. Every adult with the capacity to influence a child's educational trajectory has access to the same evidence. The question is no longer *what is happening* — it is *who is accountable for allowing it to continue*.

Three crises have converged inside American higher education. Each is serious alone. Together, they constitute a systemic failure to prepare human minds for the world those minds will actually inhabit.

**The prestige copying crisis.** Colleges reward precisely the cognitive skills that machines have permanently mastered — bounded, rule-complete, automatically-gradable performance — because the prestige system directing educational investment operates through status copying, not independent evaluation of what generates human value. The copying mechanism was adaptive. The environment has changed. The copying continues.

**The credential inflation crisis.** Elite universities have inflated their grading systems to the point of epistemic collapse. At Harvard, solid A's comprised 60 percent of all undergraduate letter grades in 2025 — up from 24 percent in 2005. A credential that once signaled genuine distinction now signals completion. The prestige of the institution fills the gap where the information content of the degree used to be.

**The character formation crisis.** The consumer university model — in which students are understood as customers purchasing a product — has systematically dismantled the conditions under which the non-cognitive skills essential to human capability development actually form. What a tiny cattle ranch in the California desert does naturally and necessarily, America's elite institutions have designed out of the experience.

These three crises share a root cause: the prestige system directing educational investment is no longer tracking what it claims to track. It is measuring and rewarding the wrong things, inflating the currency with which it measures them, and failing to create the conditions under which the right things develop. The institutions that should correct this failure are instead maintaining it. That is not an accident. It is the predictable output of a prestige copying mechanism operating without independent evaluation of the signal it transmits.

This document maps the mechanism with precision, grounds it in the best available research across evolutionary anthropology, cognitive science, economics, and machine learning evaluation, and specifies what responsible disruption looks like — and what moral obligations it generates.

## 1. The Benchmark Record: The Map of What Machines Have Won

The AI evaluation community maintains a public record of saturated benchmarks — cognitive domains where frontier machine performance has rendered human differentiation economically irrelevant. When a benchmark saturates, the evaluation community discards it and constructs a harder one. The graveyard of saturated benchmarks is therefore a precise, empirically verified map of cognitive territory that machines have permanently annexed.

### The Saturation Record (2026)

| Benchmark | Domain | Score at Launch | 2026 Score | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| GSM8K | Grade-school arithmetic | ~35% (GPT-3) | ~99% | Solved |
| HellaSwag | Commonsense sentence completion | ~40% | >95% | Solved |
| HumanEval | Python code generation | ~28% | ~93% | Solved |
| MATH-500 | Competition mathematics | <10% | ~96% | Solved |
| MMLU | Broad academic knowledge (57 subjects) | ~35% | >90% | Saturated |
| MMLU-Pro | Graduate multi-choice reasoning | ~70% | ~90% | Near-saturated |

Stanford HAI (2026) identifies SWE-bench Verified as the most dramatic single-year capability step in benchmark history: from 60% to near 100% of human expert performance within twelve months. This benchmark tests the actual operational task of professional software engineers — not toy problems, but real GitHub issues with deployable fixes.

### The Evaluation Community's Verdict on Spelling and Chess

Spelling bee performance and chess calculation do not appear in this table. They are absent not because they are easy but because they were beneath the evaluation floor before transformer architectures existed. The research community considered them solved and moved on. America's prestige system continued awarding trophies.

The benchmark saturation record is public, updated in real time, and requires no technical expertise to read. It is not consulted by the institutions that design the prestige signals directing children's cognitive investment.

## 2. Prestige-Biased Transmission: The Evolutionary Architecture

### The Mechanism

Henrich & Gil-White (2001) establish prestige-biased cultural transmission as the foundational mechanism of human cultural learning. Unlike dominance hierarchies maintained by force, prestige hierarchies are freely conferred by observers who judge an individual's competence and willingly copy that individual's behavior, preferences, and choices.

The evolutionary logic is sound: in a stable environment where adaptive knowledge is costly to acquire independently, copying the choices of demonstrably skilled individuals is a low-cost, high-accuracy heuristic. The heuristic works when prestige and genuine competence are correlated. It fails when the environment changes faster than the prestige system updates — when the prestigious individuals are skilled at the old game and the new game has different rules.

Three properties of prestige-biased copying define the current failure:

**Generalization beyond the prestige domain.** Observers copy not only the specific behaviors that generated the target's prestige but generalize to adjacent preferences and valuations. A chess grandmaster who visibly values analytical discipline confers prestige on analytical discipline broadly — including in contexts where the grandmaster has no expertise to evaluate whether that achievement develops human-differentiated capability.

**Attention competition.** Prestige-biased copying generates attention competition around high-status targets. Institutions actively seek proximity to prestigious signals. A prestige signal slightly misaligned with genuine value gets amplified before the misalignment is detected, because the detection mechanism — independent evaluation of signal validity — is bypassed by the copying heuristic itself.

**Lag in signal updating.** Because prestige signals are maintained by collective consensus, they require collective revision. An individual who correctly perceives that a prestige signal has become disconnected from genuine value faces an asymmetric cost structure: defecting from the consensus imposes immediate social costs while the benefits of reallocation are diffuse and long-term. The signal persists past its validity through this structural coordination failure.

### The Current Failure Mode

The environment has changed faster than the prestige system can update. Machine learning benchmarks document the precise capability trajectory. The prestige system has not incorporated this information, because prestige systems do not update through benchmark leaderboards — they update through high-status individuals publicly revising their behavior. Those individuals have not revised. The copying continues. The environment no longer rewards what the prestige signals encode.

## 3. The Prestige Lag: Documented Cases of Status Surviving Obsolescence

### The Arithmetic Calculation Precedent

The pocket calculator achieved commercial ubiquity by the mid-1970s. Human speed arithmetic lost all practical economic value within a decade. Educational reallocation was complete within approximately one generation: mental arithmetic curricula were reduced, replaced by mathematical reasoning, proof, and applied quantitative thinking. No competitive speed arithmetic tournaments receive federal sponsorship today. The reallocation happened, but required roughly fifteen years and deliberate institutional decisions by educators who publicly revised their judgment.

### The Slide Rule Case

Slide rule proficiency was through the 1960s a genuine professional differentiator in engineering. It required training, practice, and skill. It was taught in schools and tested in professional examinations. The first scientific calculators rendered it obsolete within five years. The prestige signal collapsed rapidly because the affected community — engineers and scientists — had direct, unmediated contact with the substituting technology. They used it and knew.

### Why the Current Cases Are Slower

Chess and spelling occupy a structurally different position. The prestige signals for both are maintained not primarily by practitioners with direct contact with the substituting technology, but by:

- Parents who do not routinely use frontier AI systems
- Educational institutions whose assessment frameworks predate the current capability trajectory
- Media organizations covering competitions as human interest stories
- Corporate sponsors whose decisions are made by marketing departments, not AI researchers

The feedback loop between capability milestone and prestige signal revision is broken at each of these nodes. No professional community with direct exposure to the substituting technology maintains the signal. It persists in a cultural vacuum, insulated from the information that would trigger revision.

### The Time Constraint

The World Economic Forum Future of Jobs Report (2025) projects that 40% of job skills will change within five years. The arithmetic calculator reallocation took fifteen years in a period of slow technological change. The current reallocation must happen in a fraction of that time against accelerating capability advancement. The prestige system's lag time is structurally mismatched to the current rate of change.

## 4. The Copying Chain: How the Signal Propagates

The prestige copying mechanism operates through identifiable chains of status transmission. Each link is a specific decision by a specific agent.

```
Elite University Admissions Signal
         ↓  copies and amplifies
Corporate Sponsorship and Media Coverage
         ↓  copies and amplifies
High School Counselor Recommendation
         ↓  copies and amplifies
Parent Resource Allocation
         ↓  copies and amplifies
Child Cognitive Investment
         ↓
Misallocated Human Capability
```

### Each Amplification Node

**University admissions.** The highest-leverage node. Every downstream agent — counselors, parents, students — partly calibrates to the admissions signal. When elite universities treat spelling bee championships and chess ratings as positive intellectual signals, they endorse the underlying prestige claim. A signal originating in one admissions office propagates through every guidance counselor, tutor market, and parental conversation in the country.

**Corporate sponsorship.** Provides institutional laundering for the prestige signal. Parents observing major corporate sponsorship infer that resource-rich, forward-looking institutions have evaluated the activity's value. The inference is incorrect; the information structure of the signal makes the error invisible.

**Media coverage.** National spelling bees and chess championships are covered as celebrations of discipline and intellectual seriousness, framing the deliberate exclusion of AI tools as the story's moral center. The coverage is accurate about what it observes. It is systematically incomplete about what it omits: any comparison with the benchmark record documenting that these domains were machine-solved before transformer architectures existed.

**Professional coaching markets.** A market of professional spelling bee and chess coaches exists precisely because the prestige signal is strong enough to generate willingness to pay. The existence of the coaching market itself becomes a prestige signal — visible investment by other serious parents is evidence that the domain warrants investment. The cascade is self-amplifying.

**The self-referential property.** The copying chain is stable because each node's behavior provides apparent evidence that the others have independently evaluated the underlying value. Sunstein & Thaler (2008) identify this as the standard structure of an informational cascade: collective behavior that appears to reflect distributed expert judgment but in fact reflects distributed copying of a common prior. Correction requires a shock from outside the cascade — a source of information that cascade participants cannot simply absorb and copy.

## 5. Grade Inflation as Prestige Laundering: The Harvard Case

### The Collapse of the Signal

On May 20, 2026, Harvard's Faculty of Arts and Sciences voted 458 to 201 to cap the number of A grades awarded to undergraduates. Starting in fall 2027, courses will limit A grades to 20 percent of enrollment plus four additional A's per class. The vote was preceded by months of intense campus debate and a student survey in which 85 percent of respondents rejected the grading cap.

The reform was driven by a single, damning number: solid A's comprised **60 percent** of all Harvard undergraduate letter grades in 2025, up from just 24 percent in 2005. In twenty years, the share of grades awarded at the highest level more than doubled. The credential that once signaled extraordinary distinction had become the expected outcome of enrollment at a selective institution.

The grading subcommittee's statement upon passing the vote was unambiguous: "A Harvard A grade will now tell them, as well as employers and graduate schools, something real about what a student has achieved. An A will once again be what Harvard's guidelines have long said it is: a mark of extraordinary distinction." That this statement needed to be made — that it described a future aspiration rather than a present reality — is the measure of how completely the signal had collapsed.

Harvard's Dean of Undergraduate Education Amanda Claybaugh described grade inflation as "a widespread problem in the Ivy League and in colleges across the country" and expressed hope that "other universities will follow."

### Grade Inflation as Prestige Laundering

The economic function of a grade is signal transmission: communicating genuine information about a student's capability to downstream evaluators. When 60 percent of grades carry the same value as what was once reserved for extraordinary performance, the grade has been laundered of its information content.

What replaces the information content? Institutional prestige. The Harvard degree still carries reputational weight — weight accumulated over decades in which the credential genuinely distinguished its recipients. That accumulated prestige masks the degradation of the underlying signal. This is a form of reputational capital depletion: the institution is spending credibility accumulated under a higher-information regime to sustain a lower-information credential. The depletion is invisible to recipients of the signal — the prestige laundering works precisely because institutional reputation operates on longer timescales than grade inflation.

### The Becker Framework: Three Failure Modes

Becker's (1964) foundational theory of human capital distinguishes depreciation (skills decay without practice) from obsolescence (skills lose market value when technology changes the production function). Grade inflation creates a third failure mode that Becker's framework did not anticipate: **signal degradation**, in which the credential that was meant to communicate skill acquisition loses its information content through institutional inflation, independently of whether the underlying skill has depreciated or been rendered obsolete.

Harvard's grading reform is an attempt to restore signal integrity. The student population voted 85 percent against it. The students' preference reveals the precise nature of the consumer university relationship: students prefer the inflated credential to the honest one, because the inflated credential carries institutional prestige without requiring genuine distinction.

### The Restuccia-Rogerson Implication

Restuccia & Rogerson (2017) establish that factor misallocation — routing high-productivity inputs to low-productivity uses — is a primary driver of total factor productivity gaps across economies. Grade inflation is a misallocation mechanism at the signal level: it routes evaluators' attention and institutional confidence toward a credential that no longer carries the information it claims to carry. The aggregate productivity loss compounds as the degraded signal propagates through hiring decisions, graduate admissions, and self-assessments across millions of students and evaluators.

## 6. The Consumer University and the Collapse of Character Formation

### The Consumer Model

Writing in *The New York Times* (May 20, 2026), Michal Leibowitz identifies the root pathology of American higher education not in individual bad actors but in a structural orientation: the consumer university, in which students understand themselves as purchasing a product and the institution as the service provider.

Leibowitz documents the consequences with a precise, diagnostic image: college students who would take dining hall dishes to their rooms and throw them in the trash rather than return them, because "the cost of dining hall dishes was not even a rounding error in the university's huge budget. No one suffered from their loss. The action landed without ripples." The students were not sociopathic. They were behaving consistently with the model the institution had implicitly endorsed: the university exists to serve them; they have no reciprocal obligations to it; optimizing for personal outcomes is not just acceptable but the point.

### What the Consumer Model Cannot Develop

Peterson's formal model identifies unpriced non-cognitive skills as the category of educational output most endangered by AI-driven educational optimization. These include:

- Persistence under genuine uncertainty
- Tolerance for productive struggle
- Self-regulation in the absence of external accountability structures
- Epistemic humility in the face of complex, ambiguous problems
- Sense of obligation to a community beyond oneself

These traits do not develop through consumption of educational services. They develop through genuine responsibility — through conditions in which one's actions have real consequences for others, in which difficulty cannot be outsourced, and in which the community's functioning depends on one's actual performance rather than one's purchased credentials.

The consumer university is architecturally designed to prevent these conditions. Fleets of janitors remove the consequences of carelessness. Bureaucratized accommodation systems convert genuine limitation into procedural workarounds. Grade inflation removes the consequence of insufficient effort. Every friction point at which character might be tested and developed has been smoothed away in service of the customer experience.

Leibowitz diagnoses the result: institutions that "claim to foster community" but "in reality, are opaque, bureaucratized, customer-service institutions that offer students little stake in a common life." Gaming systems to gain an edge is "becoming one of the best-learned lessons of an elite college education."

### The MacIntyre Dimension

The NYT piece invokes Alasdair MacIntyre's observation that one of the defining moments in the transition to modernity was when people began working outside the household — severing the direct connection between individual labor and the sustaining of the community. Before that severance, it was "easy and right" to understand "work as part of the sustaining of the community of the household and of the wider forms of community which the household in turn sustains."

The consumer university has recreated the post-severance condition at institutional scale. Students work — they study, they perform — but their work is disconnected from any community they actually sustain. The dormitories are cleaned by someone else. The food is prepared by someone else. The infrastructure is maintained by someone else. The student's contribution to the common life is zero, by design.

Hartmut Rosa's sociology of resonance offers a complementary diagnosis: genuine human flourishing requires a "responsive relationship to the world" in which "we are touched by it and answer it in turn." The consumer university produces the opposite — a transactional relationship in which the world (the institution) is there to serve the student, and the student is there to extract maximum value. Alienation is not a side effect. It is the designed output of the consumer model.

### The Virtue Ethics Verdict

Aristotelian ethics holds that virtues are developed through practice in appropriate activities. The virtues essential to human capability in an AI-intensive economy — epistemic humility, adversarial self-scrutiny, tolerance for productive ambiguity, genuine accountability to others — cannot develop in an institution architecturally designed to eliminate the conditions under which those virtues are tested. The consumer university is not merely failing to develop character. It is actively preventing its development by removing every situation in which character would be required.

## 7. Deep Springs: The Existence Proof

Against the backdrop of Harvard's grading crisis and Leibowitz's anatomy of the consumer university, Deep Springs College functions as an existence proof: the conditions for genuine character formation and human-differentiated capability development can be institutionally designed. They are not utopian aspirations.

### The Institution

Founded in 1917 by hydroelectric tycoon L.L. Nunn, Deep Springs educates approximately 26 students each year, offering a free two-year liberal arts education on a working cattle ranch forty miles from Bishop, California. Students serve as cowboys and butchers, sit on the board of trustees, staff the curriculum committee, run a volunteer fire department, and clean the wounds of dairy cows. The labor program is valued most for its pedagogical function: work allows students to contemplate their role in a community, to practice working hard, and to foster a sense of ownership and responsibility.

Students at Deep Springs learn to see themselves not as consumers of a degree — an individual good — but as creators of an education, a collective good.

### What Deep Springs Develops That Elite Universities Do Not

The contrast with the consumer university is total and deliberate. Where the consumer university removes consequences, Deep Springs requires them. Rebecca McMillin-Hastings, describing cleaning an infected wound on a dairy cow: "You just kind of have to get your soap in your water and, just like, push on the wound. And it really hurts her." She described throwing her entire body weight against the animal, knowing that she was hurting her, feeling that she was hurting her, but also knowing that it had to be done.

That cognitive and affective experience — acting under genuine stakes, with irreducible consequence, in a situation that resists being reduced to a rubric — is what Aristotle means by the conditions necessary for virtue development. Deep Springs has designed it in. American elite universities have designed it out.

For small and large institutions alike, the question Deep Springs poses is this: *For what came ye into the wilderness?* Not for conventional scholastic training; not to become proficient in commercial or professional pursuits for personal gain. You came to prepare for a life of service, with the understanding that superior ability and generous purpose would be expected of you.

### Why Deep Springs Is Illegible to the Prestige System

Deep Springs is, by every measurable metric, a prestigious educational institution: its graduates go on to elite universities, its alumni include accomplished figures across fields, and its intellectual culture is by reported accounts formidable. But the prestige system has no mechanism for registering what Deep Springs actually does well — because what it does well produces no trophies, no rankings, no legible credential that can be copied by observers using the prestige-biased transmission heuristic.

A parent cannot explain Deep Springs to a neighbor in the same sentence it takes to explain "my child won the National Spelling Bee." The value is real. The signal is invisible. The prestige copying mechanism cannot copy what it cannot see.

## 8. Peterson's Model: Why the Distortion Worsens

Peterson (2025), *Training for Obsolescence? The AI-Driven Education Trap* (arXiv:2508.19625), provides the formal economic model that makes the prestige copying problem quantifiable and its trajectory predictable.

### The Core Finding

A pre-registered pilot study documents a positive correlation between a skill's amenability to AI-assisted teaching and its vulnerability to AI-driven workplace substitution. This correlation is not empirically contingent — it is structurally necessary, following from the architectural properties of AI systems.

Skills that AI can teach efficiently share the same structural properties as skills AI can perform at zero marginal cost. Both require:

- **Rule-completeness** — all relevant rules are finite and specifiable
- **Algorithmic tractability** — the solution space can be searched systematically
- **Rubric-gradability** — correct answers can be verified without human judgment

These properties make a domain excellent for AI-assisted teaching and simultaneously make it trivially automatable. The AI teaching tool and the AI that replaces the human practitioner are solving the same structural problem.

### The Monotonic Distortion

The model's key structural result: the skill mismatch between what educational systems train and what the labor market rewards **increases monotonically with AI prevalence**. As AI teaching tools become more capable, they teach the wrong skills more efficiently. The pedagogical gain at the classroom level directly amplifies the economic displacement at the labor market level.

An institution that deploys AI to make its existing curriculum more efficient is not modernizing. It is accelerating the misallocation.

### The Non-Cognitive Crowding-Out

Peterson's most consequential extension: when AI shortcuts reduce the difficulty of tasks — when a student can produce a credentialed output without the struggle that historically developed persistence, self-regulation, and epistemic humility — educational systems optimizing for measured outcomes will systematically under-invest in the conditions that develop them. The loss does not register in institutional metrics. It registers, eventually, in the labor market and in the character of the people who navigated the system.

Deep Springs produces the opposite: conditions so resistant to shortcut that the non-cognitive skills cannot help but develop. The distillation of human capability that L.L. Nunn designed in 1917 anticipated, without knowing it, exactly the educational target that the AI economy demands.

### Peterson's Policy Implication

AI adoption in education must be paired with explicit forward-looking labor market signals, because the signals that would naturally correct the misallocation are structurally absent. No futures market exists for the wage-suppressing effect of current AI systems on the skills being taught today. Educational planners observe the immediate productivity benefit of AI-assisted teaching; they do not observe the future displacement cost. The prestige system, the primary mechanism transmitting educational investment signals to parents, does not incorporate this information at all.

## 9. The Jagged Frontier: Where Human Capability Actually Lives

The Stanford HAI 2026 AI Index introduces the jagged frontier as the defining structural feature of current machine capability. The same frontier models that won gold at the International Mathematical Olympiad read analog clocks correctly only 50.1% of the time. Models that saturate graduate-level structured reasoning benchmarks fail consistently on tasks requiring genuine ambiguity navigation, multi-stakeholder synthesis, and long-horizon planning in novel environments.

### The Shape of the Gap

This jaggedness is not a temporary engineering limitation. It is the structural shape of the capability boundary — the consequence of what large language models are and how they work. LLMs are extraordinarily powerful interpolators within the distribution of their training data. They face a genuine structural challenge at the open-world boundary: tasks that require constructing the relevant variable space, not operating within a given one.

|---|---|---|
| Open-world problem framing | Requires constructing variable space, not searching within it | Kapoor et al. (2026) open-world evaluation failures |
| Multi-stakeholder synthesis | Novel minds diverge from training distribution | Ledingham et al. (2025), UK GDS task reallocation |

### Humanity's Last Exam

Phan et al. (2026), *Nature*: Humanity's Last Exam (HLE) comprises 2,500 questions across mathematics, natural sciences, and humanities, contributed by approximately 1,000 domain experts from 500+ institutions in 50 countries. Frontier models score 35–53%. Human domain experts score approximately 74% within their own specialty.

HLE tests the capabilities at the open-world, novel-synthesis, expert-judgment boundary where human premium currently concentrates. Harvard's grading reform, the consumer university's customer-experience optimization, and the prestige copying machine's trophy architecture have all produced graduates with no systematic training in any of these capabilities. The credential is inflated. The capability is missing.

## 10. Bourdieu's Field Theory: Why the Educational Field Cannot Self-Correct

### Fields, Capital, and Habitus

Bourdieu's *Distinction* (1984) and *The Field of Cultural Production* (1993) analyze prestige as symbolic capital — a resource generating advantage through recognition rather than intrinsic productive capacity. Symbolic capital is field-specific: the prestige of a chess championship carries weight within the educational and parenting fields even though it generates no premium in the labor market field.

Fields reproduce themselves through the habitus of participants: embodied dispositions, perceptions, and valuations that feel natural and obvious to those who inhabit the field. An admissions officer who has spent decades in the educational field has a habitus that registers spelling bee achievement as obviously valuable. The perception is not a deliberate calculation. It is a structural effect of field membership.

### The Doxa Problem

Bourdieu identifies doxa as assumptions so deeply embedded in a field that they are not available for questioning — they are simply how the world obviously is. Within the educational field, the assumption that performance in precisely-specified, rubric-gradable, competitive academic tasks signals intellectual seriousness is doxic. It is not defended; it is presupposed.

Challenging doxic assumptions requires importing tools from outside the field — labor economics, machine learning evaluation, cognitive science — that the field's habitus does not naturally incorporate. This is why benchmark saturation records do not automatically revise educational prestige signals. The benchmark record is produced in a different field, with different legitimating institutions. Translation requires individuals with credibility in both fields — a structurally rare combination.

### The Yale Self-Critique as Inadequate Response

In April 2026, a committee of Yale professors published an unusually self-critical report urging the school to protect free speech, make higher education more affordable, and streamline bureaucracy. Leibowitz characterizes these reflections as "a start" that "ignore a core problem at the root of so many campus and social issues" — namely, the collapse of communal obligation and the dominance of the consumer model.

The Yale committee could not easily answer the question: "What share of Yale's resources is devoted to its core academic functions and what share is not?" This is not bureaucratic incompetence. It is the Bourdieusian field reproducing its own opacity: the field's internal legitimating apparatus does not require that question to be answerable, because the field's value is maintained by prestige consensus, not by independent audit of whether the functions justify the prestige.

## 11. The Teachability-Substitutability Correlation: The Central Mechanism

### Why the Correlation Is Structural

The positive correlation between AI teachability and AI substitutability follows necessarily from the architectural properties of AI systems. An AI teaching tool and the AI system that replaces the human practitioner succeed for the same structural reasons and fail for the same structural reasons. They are the same type of system solving the same type of problem.

This means that any educational institution tracking the teachability signal — deploying AI tools to teach more efficiently — is systematically investing in the skills that AI will perform most cheaply. The efficiency gain at the pedagogical level is real. The displacement at the labor market level is guaranteed by the same structural properties that produced the efficiency gain.

### The Corollary

Skills that resist AI-assisted teaching resist full automation for the same structural reasons. Tasks involving genuine ambiguity, novel stakeholder modeling, open-world problem framing, and epistemic audit of probabilistic outputs resist efficient AI teaching precisely because they lack the properties that make AI teaching effective: clear practice problems, automated feedback, rubric-measurable mastery.

This corollary defines the training target with precision. The skills hardest to teach with AI tools are, structurally, the skills hardest for AI to perform. An educational system tracking this signal would systematically target human-differentiated capability. Harvard's 60 percent A rate, the consumer university's customer-service architecture, and the spelling bee prestige apparatus all invert this logic entirely.

### The Prestige System as Automatable-Skill Detector

Any skill that the educational prestige system celebrates — through competition structure, grade rewards, admissions weighting, or media coverage — is almost certainly a skill with high AI teachability and therefore high AI substitutability. The prestige system was designed for a world where measurable educational outcomes and genuinely valuable educational outcomes were the same thing. They diverged when AI became capable of performing the measurable ones. The prestige system has not registered the divergence. Grade inflation has made the divergence worse by inflating the credential that represented the measurable outcomes while leaving the genuinely valuable ones untracked.

## 12. The Effort Visibility Trap: Why Wrong Skills Look Right

### The Visibility Structure of Saturated Skills

Spelling bee performance and chess ratings share a cluster of properties that make them appear maximally serious as cognitive endeavors within the prestige copying framework:

- **Public performance** — failure and success occur before observers
- **Emotional salience** — the stakes are high, the feedback is immediate, the emotions are visible
- **Legible difficulty** — thousands of hours of drilling; the investment is obvious and quantifiable
- **Binary feedback** — correct or incorrect, no ambiguity in the evaluation
- **Persistent credential** — rankings, trophies, titles that can be cited and copied

These properties are ideal for prestige signal transmission. The signal is accurate within its domain. The problem is that the domain is wrong.

Grade inflation compounds this problem. A Harvard A is a legible, persistent credential — even when it represents only average performance within an institution that has inflated 60 percent of its grades to that level. The credential is copied by downstream evaluators who cannot independently assess what it represents. Prestige launders the inflated signal into an apparently trustworthy one.

### The Invisibility of Human-Premium Skills

The skills concentrating human cognitive premium in an AI-intensive economy are systematically low-visibility by structural necessity:

**Epistemic auditing** produces no trophy. Detecting that an AI has fabricated a protein interaction or hallucinated a legal citation requires domain knowledge and calibrated uncertainty — invisible cognitive work that leaves no legible artifact.

**Structural problem framing** produces a document that non-experts cannot evaluate without the domain knowledge to assess whether the variable identification was appropriate and the constraint specification was coherent.

**Adversarial self-reasoning** is entirely internal. It leaves no visible artifact. Its quality can only be observed in downstream analytical quality — several inferential steps removed from the skill itself.

**Agentic system architecture** produces a workflow. The cognitive skill that produced it is unobservable to the prestige copying mechanism.

Prestige-biased copying cannot attach to invisible skills. The same structural properties that make a domain accessible to AI — rule-completeness, algorithmic tractability, automated gradability — make performance in that domain highly visible and legible. The properties that make a domain resistant to AI make performance low-visibility and difficult for non-experts to evaluate. The prestige system is structurally biased toward automatable skills. Not through malice. Through the geometry of visibility.

## 13. Mimetic Contagion: How Corporate Sponsorship Amplifies the Signal

### Girard's Mimetic Framework

René Girard's mimetic theory (*Deceit, Desire, and the Novel*, 1961) holds that human desire is fundamentally mimetic — we desire what we see others desiring, particularly high-status others. The prestige signal of the spelling bee is not primarily evaluated by parents asking "does this develop genuine human capability?" It is experienced as a transmission of high-status desire: *these impressive, successful people want their children to do this; therefore this is what serious parents do.*

In a stable environment where high-status individuals' preferences reliably guide toward genuine value, this is an efficient heuristic. The mechanism fails when high-status individuals' preferences have not updated to the current environment.

### The Corporate Amplification Mechanism

A corporation sponsoring a national spelling bee is expressing institutional desire — treating the competition as worthy of resources, attention, and brand association. For an observing parent, the sponsorship is processed as evidence that intelligent, resource-rich, forward-looking institutional actors have evaluated the activity and found it worthwhile. The parent is wrong to draw this inference: the sponsorship decision was made by a marketing department pursuing brand alignment with perceived parental values, not by an AI capability research team. But the information structure of the signal makes this impossible to observe.

### The Self-Referential Cascade

The result is mimetic contagion: the prestige signal jumps from the educational field to the corporate field and back, gaining authority at each jump. Parents copy educators who copy university admissions who copy corporate sponsors who are themselves copying the parental behavior that drives their consumer demographics. No node in the system is performing an independent evaluation of the underlying value. Each node assumes the others have.

This is the standard structure of an informational cascade (Sunstein & Thaler, 2008). Each participant assumes other participants have evaluated the underlying value independently. In fact, most participants are inferring value from other participants' behavior. The cascade persists far past the point where independent evaluation would have triggered revision.

## 14. The Four Structural Moral Failures

The prestige copying mechanism is maintained by four identifiable structural failures, each with identifiable agents.

### Failure I — The Effort-Outcome Confusion

Adults systematically reward visible effort rather than productive effort. A child who drills 40,000 words demonstrates effort that is highly visible: the hours are long, the feedback is immediate, the performance is public and emotionally charged. A child who develops epistemic auditing capacity demonstrates effort that is largely invisible: reading research, testing AI outputs against domain knowledge, maintaining calibration records, building mental models of failure modes.

The first child gets a trophy. The second gets nothing a parent can put on a shelf.

This is a systematic misalignment between reward and value that is maintained, renewed, and amplified by adult decisions. Adults who reward visible effort over productive effort are teaching children that the former is what matters. They are wrong. The children pay for the error.

### Failure II — The Prestige Inertia Problem

Prestige attaches to pathways that have historically filtered for demonstrated ability. Spelling bees and chess tournaments have filtered for talented, disciplined children for generations. The filtering function was real. But the filtering function is now severed from its referent: the skills being filtered for no longer map to human cognitive comparative advantage.

Adults who maintain prestige signals past the point where they track genuine value are not merely making an error. They are actively deceiving the children whose behavior they are shaping. A prestige signal that once meant "this child is developing capabilities that will generate value" and now means "this child is optimizing for a domain that machines dominate" is a false signal. Adults who issue it, repeat it, and reward it bear responsibility for that deception.

### Failure III — The Status Laundering Problem

A specific and pervasive form of the moral failure is the use of children's achievement as a vehicle for adult status. When a parent publicizes a spelling bee championship, the primary beneficiary of the attention is the parent. The child provides the performance; the parent captures the reputational return.

This becomes morally problematic when the adult's reputational interest in a visible, legible achievement overrides genuine inquiry into whether that achievement serves the child's development. The parent who would be embarrassed to tell their social circle that their child spent three years building epistemic auditing skills — because the skill is invisible, unjudgeable, and trophy-less — is prioritizing their own social comfort over their child's genuine formation. That is a moral failure with a name: vanity, in the specific sense that Aristotelian ethics gives to the vice of prioritizing appearance over reality.

### Failure IV — The Epistemic Negligence Problem

Adults who make significant resource allocation decisions on behalf of children have a duty of epistemic diligence proportionate to the stakes. The stakes here are high: thousands of hours of a child's cognitive development during the most plastic years of their life.

An adult who allocates $30,000 in tutoring fees and 2,000 hours of a child's time to a competitive activity without consulting the available research on whether that activity develops human-differentiated capability is not exercising due diligence. The research exists. The benchmark saturation record is public. The labor market projections are published. The adult who does not consult them is engaging in epistemic negligence: the culpable failure to gather available evidence before making decisions with significant consequences for others.

Epistemic negligence does not excuse the agent. It compounds the fault.

## 15. The Moral Case: Culpability, Phronesis, and Adult Obligation

### The Knowability Condition

Moral culpability typically requires the capacity to know and the failure to act on available knowledge. Both conditions are satisfied here.

The benchmark saturation record is public. The Peterson model is peer-reviewed and available. The Harvard grade inflation data — 24 percent A's in 2005, 60 percent in 2025 — is published in the institution's own reports. The information required to recognize that current prestige signals are miscalibrated does not require technical expertise. It requires the decision to look.

Aquinas's treatment of vincible ignorance is directly relevant: ignorance that results from insufficient diligence, when diligence was both possible and required by the stakes, does not excuse the agent. It compounds the fault. A parent who allocates $30,000 in coaching fees and 2,000 hours of a child's cognitive development to a competitive domain without consulting the publicly available record of machine capability in that domain is engaging in epistemic negligence.

### The Virtue Ethics Case: Phronesis

The Aristotelian virtue most directly implicated is phronesis — practical wisdom, the capacity to discern the right action in particular circumstances. Aristotle treats the rearing of children as the paradigm case of phronesis in action, grounding parental authority in the child's incapacity for full rational agency. The parent exercises judgment on behalf of someone who cannot yet exercise it themselves. This trusteeship relationship creates a correspondingly elevated duty.

Phronesis requires perception of particulars — seeing the relevant features of the specific situation rather than applying general rules blindly. The relevant particular here is the benchmark saturation record. A parent of practical wisdom, confronted with evidence that a machine executes a skill at zero marginal cost with perfect accuracy, would ask: what is the point of training my child to do this? The phronimos does not need a philosopher to answer. The answer is visible to anyone who looks.

The parent who does not look — who accepts the prestige signal of the spelling bee trophy without examining whether the skill it rewards generates human value — is failing the Aristotelian test. They are substituting conventional approval for practical wisdom. Aristotle names this vice: doxophilia, the love of reputation over the love of the good.

### The Deontological Case: Children as Ends

Kantian ethics grounds parental obligation in the child's status as a rational being in development. The categorical imperative, in its second formulation — treat rational beings always as ends, never merely as means — applies with particular force to the parent-child relationship. Genuine parental care is directed at the child's actual flourishing. Corrupted care substitutes the parent's desire for social validation for the child's genuine interest.

Could a parent universalize the maxim "I will direct my child toward activities that maximize visible achievement signals, without examining whether those signals track genuine human capability in the current technological environment"? The universalized world is one in which an entire generation is trained for obsolescence while the machines that have made their training obsolete become more capable every quarter.

### The Consequentialist Case: Quantified Harm

The individual child whose training concentrates in saturated capability domains enters an economy where those skills carry no wage premium above the automated baseline. The median competitive spelling bee participant spends 1,000–3,000 hours in preparation. At the opportunity cost of equivalent time invested in agentic system architecture, epistemic auditing, or structural problem framing, the loss is concrete and large.

At the aggregate, Restuccia & Rogerson's (2017) analysis establishes the macroeconomic stakes. When a nation's most motivated, disciplined, high-potential adolescents concentrate their cognitive investment in domains that AI executes at zero cost, the foregone R&D capacity, institutional design capability, and systems-level thinking that those hours could have produced represents a macro-level drag that accumulates across cohorts.

A common evasion in cases of structural harm is the diffusion of responsibility across "the system." This evasion is not available here. Parents who fund the coaching, educators who weight the achievement, admissions officers who treat these as positive intellectual signals, corporate sponsors, media organizations — each makes a discrete, revisable decision. None is compelled by the structure of the world to make the decision they are making.

## 16. What Morally Adequate Adult Behavior Looks Like

Moral obligation is not discharged by diagnosis alone. The following constitutes the minimum threshold of morally adequate adult behavior in the current environment.

### For Parents

**Epistemic diligence.** Before allocating significant resources to a child's cognitive training — money, time, opportunity cost — consult the available evidence on whether that domain develops human-differentiated capability. The benchmark saturation record is publicly available. Ignorance of available evidence is not a defense.

**The phronesis question.** For any competitive or intensive training activity, ask: could a frontier AI system execute this task at performance levels exceeding 90% of trained human competitors? If yes, examine whether the genuine developmental value — persistence, discipline, competitive resilience — could be routed through a vehicle that points at the right cognitive targets. The answer is almost always yes. The routing is the parent's responsibility.

**Separate effort from outcome.** Praise and reward the character traits that intensive training develops — persistence, discipline, tolerance for struggle — while being honest about whether the specific vehicle is developing capability that will matter. The child can be told: you are building real qualities through this work. We are going to make sure those qualities are pointed in the right direction.

**The status audit.** Examine honestly whether a child's activity is being maintained partly because it generates social recognition for the parent. If it is, that is not automatically disqualifying — but it is a conflict of interest that must be consciously managed. The child's developmental interest, not the parent's reputational interest, is the decision criterion.

### For Educators

**The diagnostic question.** Apply annually to every major curriculum component: can a frontier AI system execute this learning objective at performance levels exceeding 90% of students completing the current curriculum? If yes, the component is a candidate for redesign — not elimination, but redesign toward a vehicle that develops the same character traits while targeting human-differentiated cognitive capabilities.

**Reform assessment.** Introduce assessment components that are structurally impossible to outsource to AI: open-world problem framing, epistemic audit portfolios, multi-stakeholder communication records, adversarial reasoning documentation. These reveal genuine cognitive capability in ways that vocabulary drills and chess ratings do not.

**Honest prestige signals.** An educator who nominates a spelling champion for an academic achievement award is issuing a prestige signal that shapes the choices of other children and other parents. Issuing a prestige signal that encodes a false judgment about intellectual value is a professional failure.

### For Policymakers and Institutions

**Reform extracurricular weighting in admissions.** University admissions that treat spelling bee and chess achievements as intellectual markers are rewarding the wrong cognitive profile. Recognize discipline and competitive achievement through vehicles that demonstrate human-differentiated capability: open-world problem portfolios, epistemic audit records, agentic problem-solving competitions that AI proxies cannot win.

**Fund the right competitions.** The competitive apparatus is pedagogically powerful. It is pointed at the wrong target. Apply equivalent prize structures, media coverage, and institutional prestige to competitions that reward epistemic auditing, structural problem framing, and agentic system architecture.

**Connect curriculum approval to forward labor market signals.** Peterson's core policy recommendation: AI adoption in education must be paired with explicit forward-looking labor market evidence. Curriculum approval processes should require documentation of how learning objectives map to projected skill demand, with explicit exclusion of objectives falling within saturated AI capability domains.

## 17. The Human Capability Stack: What Prestige Has Not Found

The following five capabilities constitute the current human cognitive comparative advantage in an AI-intensive economy. Each is explicitly resistant to machine substitution for structural reasons. None are tracked, rewarded, or visible to the current prestige system — or to Harvard's inflated grading apparatus.

### 1. Epistemic Auditing

The structured practice of evaluating AI-generated outputs for hallucination, logical inconsistency, domain boundary violations, and structural coherence errors — distinguishing errors that matter from errors that do not.

**The economic case.** General-purpose LLMs hallucinate in 58–82% of legal queries without specialized grounding. Advanced models produce errors in 15–20% of factual citation tasks, rising to 35–55% on niche or recent topics. Analysis of NeurIPS 2025 accepted papers found confirmed hallucinated citations in approximately 53 papers despite multi-expert review. A practitioner auditing at 90% detection accuracy reduces errors by an order of magnitude over uncritical acceptance.

**Why it resists automation.** The audit of a system's outputs cannot be performed reliably by the same system. The circularity is architectural, not contingent. The Oxford semantic entropy algorithm detects approximately 79% of hallucinations in controlled experiments. Human expert validation remains the gold standard across medicine, law, science, and finance.

### 2. Structural Problem Framing

The ability to take an underspecified, ambiguous real-world situation and construct a tractable problem formulation — identifying relevant variables, binding constraints, what an adequate solution would need to satisfy, and what information would materially change the analysis.

**The economic grounding.** Acemoglu & Restrepo (2018) identify this as "expert thinking" — applying established knowledge to novel situations where the rules do not specify the answer. The situation exists; the relevant variables are not yet named. The task is to construct the specification.

**Why it resists automation.** Kapoor et al. (2026) document this as the primary failure mode of current agentic AI systems on open-world evaluations. Systems execute within a given problem structure; they fail at constructing the structure.

### 3. Agentic System Architecture

The ability to decompose complex, multi-step problems into sub-components; specify constraints, evaluation criteria, and failure modes for each; route automatable components to AI systems; and integrate partial outputs into coherent solutions meeting the original problem intent.

**The empirical anchor.** Gupta & Kumar (2026) document that agentic AI systems now execute end-to-end occupational workflows rather than discrete subtasks. The remaining human role is problem specification and integration. The SAGE framework (Elkhodr & Gide, 2025), tested across 18 student groups at four Australian universities, found that students explicitly trained in when to accept, modify, or reject AI contributions develop measurable orchestration skills that transfer across problem types. The orchestration skill is itself the non-automatable component — and the one that neither the consumer university nor the grade-inflated credential develops or tracks.

### 4. Adaptive Stakeholder Communication

The ability to accurately model the knowledge, values, incentives, and communication preferences of a novel interlocutor — and to adapt arguments and framings in real time based on evidence of comprehension, resistance, or engagement.

**Why it resists automation.** Current AI systems demonstrate significant limitations in theory-of-mind tasks when the target's mental state diverges from typical training distribution patterns. Ledingham et al. (2025) identify stakeholder management as a primary reinstatement domain — tasks that LLM-driven job redesign consistently routes back to human performers not because the technology is immature but because the task structure is misaligned with current AI optimization targets.

### 5. Adversarial Self-Reasoning

The disciplined practice of generating the strongest possible case against one's own analysis — finding the most credible ways a conclusion is wrong, the assumptions it depends on, and the conditions under which the analysis reverses.

**Why it resists automation.** AI systems trained to generate coherent, plausible content are structurally biased against invalidation — confirmation maximizes local token probability. Stanford researchers confirmed across multiple legal AI deployments that AI systems tend to agree with incorrect user premises. A practitioner with adversarial reasoning discipline generates the counter-hypothesis before accepting AI confirmation. This is the skill that Deep Springs cattle ranch conditions develop naturally: you must know you are hurting the animal and do it anyway, because you have correctly understood the situation.

## 18. The Intervention Agenda

The prestige copying chain has specific nodes. Each is a specific agent making a specific, revisable decision.

### Admissions Reform — Highest Leverage

University admissions offices are the highest-leverage node. Every downstream agent calibrates partly to the admissions signal. Harvard's grading reform is a necessary but insufficient step: restoring signal integrity in grading while continuing to reward spelling bee championships and chess ratings in admissions continues to misdirect the prestige signal toward automatable skills.

**The required revision.** Develop portfolio-based assessment components that directly test human-differentiated capability — epistemic audit portfolios, open-world problem framing submissions, agentic problem-solving records. These cannot be won by AI proxies, which is itself the evidence that they track the right capability. Weight them explicitly. Reduce or recontextualize the weight of achievements in benchmark-saturated domains. Publish the rationale — transparency generates a revised signal that propagates back through the copying chain.

**The diagnostic test.** For every extracurricular achievement currently weighted in admissions, ask: can a frontier AI system perform this task at levels exceeding 90% of trained human competitors? If yes, recalibrate the weight.

### Grade Reform as Necessary but Insufficient

Harvard's faculty vote to cap A grades at 20% of enrollment is a genuine step toward signal integrity. It addresses the credential inflation problem directly. It does not address the prestige copying problem — what the grades reward — or the character formation problem — what the institution's architecture develops.

A restored grading signal pointed at the wrong skills is marginally better than an inflated grading signal pointed at the wrong skills. The more fundamental reform is revising what the signal measures: toward the five capabilities identified in Section 17, away from the benchmark-saturated domains the prestige system currently celebrates.

### Corporate Sponsorship Redirection

The brand-alignment case for sponsoring open-world problem-solving competitions and epistemic auditing championships is at least as strong as for spelling bees — and far more defensible against the obvious critique. Competitions must be constructed so AI cannot win. That constraint is itself the signal: *this competition tests what machines cannot do.*

### Media Coverage Completion

Coverage of academic competitions should routinely include the benchmark context: what does a frontier AI system score on this task? The comparison does not diminish the child's effort. It is accurate context that the reader needs to evaluate what they are reading. The omission of this context is not dishonest — it is a failure of completeness where completeness matters.

### The Peterson Annual Protocol

Applied institutionally, Peterson's (2025) core policy recommendation becomes an operational monitoring instrument:

1. Update the benchmark saturation record annually against current evaluation leaderboards
2. Map major curriculum components and competitive recognitions to their primary cognitive domains
3. Trigger redesign review for any component whose primary domain falls within a saturated benchmark
4. Redesign asks: can the underlying discipline — persistence, rigor, competitive drive — be preserved while redirecting the vehicle toward a human-differentiated target?
5. Track the redesign ratio as a leading indicator of institutional alignment

The SAGE framework (Elkhodr & Gide, 2025) provides empirically validated curriculum redesign evidence with documented outcomes across 18 student groups at four universities.
